AQI Table

AQI Air Pollution Level Health Implications Cautionary Statement (for PM2.5)
0 - 50 Good Air quality is considered satisfactory, and air pollution poses little or no risk. None
51 - 100 Moderate Air quality is acceptable; however, for some pollutants there may be a moderate health concern for a very small number of people who are unusually sensitive to air pollution. Active children and adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
101 - 150 Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups Members of sensitive groups may experience health effects. The general public is not likely to be affected. Active children and adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
151 - 200 Unhealthy Everyone may begin to experience health effects; members of sensitive groups may experience more serious health effects. Active children and adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should avoid prolonged outdoor exertion; everyone else, especially children, should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.
201 - 300 Very Unhealthy Health warnings of emergency conditions. The entire population is more likely to be affected. Active children and adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should avoid all outdoor exertion; everyone else, especially children, should limit outdoor exertion.
300+ Hazardous Health alert: everyone may experience more serious health effects. Everyone should avoid all outdoor exertion.

Causes of Air Pollution

Deforestation

The combustion of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas in power plants, vehicles, and industrial processes releases a variety of pollutants, including sulfur dioxide (SO2), carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and particulate matter (PM). These pollutants contribute to smog, acid rain, and respiratory problems.

Industrial Emissions

Factories and industrial activities emit significant amounts of pollutants, including hydrocarbons, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon monoxide, and other chemicals. These degrade air quality and can lead to health issues such as respiratory and cardiovascular diseases.

Transportation

Vehicles, including cars, trucks, buses, and airplanes, emit pollutants such as carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen oxides (NOx), hydrocarbons, and particulate matter (PM). These emissions contribute significantly to urban air pollution and smog formation.

Agricultural Activities

Agriculture contributes to air pollution through the release of ammonia (NH3) from fertilizers and animal waste, as well as pesticides and insecticides. Additionally, practices like burning crop residues release harmful gases and particulate matter into the atmosphere.

Effects of Air Pollution

Humans

Respiratory Disease

Air pollution can cause chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, and lung cancer in humans.

Cardiovascular Diseases

Air pollution significantly impacts cardiovascular health through mechanisms involving systemic inflammation and oxidative stress.

​Cancer

​Air pollution is linked to various cancers, including lung cancer and potentially breast cancer.

Environment

Acid Rain

Air pollutants like sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) cause acid rain, which damages soil, water bodies, and plants. Acid rain makes soil acidic and harms aquatic life.

Ozone Damage

Ground-level ozone can significantly damage crops, forests, and other vegetation by reducing photosynthesis and growth rates.

​Global Warming

​Greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) contribute to global warming, leading to climate change such as rising sea levels and extreme weather events.

Prevention Method

Use of Public Transportation

Individuals can significantly reduce air pollution by adopting sustainable transportation habits. Walking or biking for short trips can greatly decrease vehicle emissions. Using public transportation like buses, trains, or subways instead of personal vehicles also helps reduce the number of cars on the road. Carpooling with fri​ends or colleagues is another effective way to lower vehicle use and cut emissions.

​Energy Conservation
​Conserving energy at home is crucial for preventing air pollution. Turning off lights and unplugging appliances when not in use saves energy and reduces emissions. Using energy-efficient products like ENERGY STAR-rated appliances and LED bulbs can significantly cut energy consumption.
​Waste Management
​Effective waste management practices are essential for reducing air pollution. Recycling and reusing materials can minimize pollution from waste processing. Composting yard waste instead of burning it prevents the release of harmful pollutants into the air. 
​Household Practices
​Using non-toxic cleaning products and low-VOC paints can reduce the release of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) into the air. Limiting the use of wood-burning fireplaces and s​toves, and considering gas logs instead, can also help reduce indoor air pollution.​ Additionally, using electric or hand-powered lawn equipment instead of gas-powered engines can significantly reduce emissions of carbon dioxide.
​Community Involvement
​Supporting clean air initiatives and participating in local programs aimed at reducing air pollution can have a profound impact. Engaging in tree planting activities not only helps absorb pollutants but also significantly improves air quality.
